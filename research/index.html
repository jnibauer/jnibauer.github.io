Statistical Properties of Elemental Abundances in Solar Analogs and Possible Links to Planet Formation

with Eric Baxter (Hawaii, IfA), Bhuvnesh Jain (UPenn), Jennifer van Saders (Hawaii, IfA), Rachael Beaton (Princeton, Carnegie Institution for Science), Johanna Teske (Carnegie Institution of Washington)

arxiv: 2010.07241 | Accepted for Publication in the Astrophysical Journal (in press)

Recent studies have demonstrated that the Sun appears depleted in refractory (rock-forming) elements by ~10% relative to nearby solar-type stars. Refractory elements form some of the main building blocks for rocky planets like our own, potentially providing a useful tracer of planet formation processes. This work utilizes stellar chemical abundance data from APOGEE and a Bayesian approach to place constraints on the global statistical properties of elemental abundances across solar analog stars, including correlations with element condensation temperature. For a population of ~1700 solar analogs, we find intriguing correlations with condensation temperature and a statistically significant bimodality in the abundance data separating refractory enriched stars from refractory depleted stars. Such trends, if robustly linked to the formation of planetary systems, provide an exciting approach to connect stellar chemical abundances to planetary properties across large samples of Milky Way stars.

The Statistics of Extended Debris Disks Measured with Gaia and Planck

with Eric Baxter (Hawaii, IfA) and Bhuvnesh Jain (UPenn)

arxiv: 1912.10498 | Accepted for Publication in the Astronomical Journal

Circumstellar disks provide key insights to planet formation processes and are linked to the discovery of nearby exoplanets. In this work, we utilize data from the Planck CMB survey correlated with stellar positions from Gaia to measure unresolved debris disks through their thermal emission at submillimeter wavelengths. By combining low signal-to-noise measurements across many stars using a novel statistical approach, we constrain the fraction of stars with disks as a function of spectral type and the shape of the underlying disk luminosity distribution. For an under sampled population of M dwarfs, we find roughly 10% of stars within 80pc have excess emission consistent with dust-dominated debris disks. Our analysis is applicable to other high resolution CMB surveys, providing an exciting new avenue to study debris disks through their thermal emission at submillimeter wavelengths over large areas.

Extra-solar Oort Clouds: Prospects for a First Detection with High Resolution CMB Surveys

with Eric Baxter (Hawaii, IfA) and Bhuvnesh Jain (UPenn)

The outer edges of the solar system are thought to be occupied by a spherical collection of icy bodies called the Oort cloud. First posited nearly 70 years ago, the Oort Cloud is believed to be the source of long-period comets observed in the inner solar system, with numerous theoretical predictions supporting its presence. To date, no direct observations of the massive extended body have been made largely due to the cloud's substantial distance from the Sun and low albedo. Because the expected temperature of the cloud is on the order of tens of Kelvins, this results in Wien peaks at millimeter and submillimeter wavelengths where the CMB is measured. By measuring massive extended Oort clouds around other stars through their thermal emission, CMB surveys provide an exciting avenue to detect a collection of exo-Oort clouds for the first time. By utilizing similar Bayesian techniques to those developed in my work on circumstellar disks, I am currently developing a novel framework to constrain, detect, and characterize the thermal emission profile of Oort clouds around distant Milky Way stars using CMB surveys. This is a challenging data-analysis task: with low signal-to-noise and relatively weak priors on the expected signal, a background model is required to precisely separate Oort cloud emission from galactic cirrus and other sources of confusion. Current and future work includes applying this framework to data from the Atacama Cosmology Telescope and the upcoming Simons Observatory CMB survey (paper in prep.).

The Generalized Stokes Theorem and Boundary Data

Paper Draft and Preliminary Results. Note, this is very much a work in progress.

Many fields require precise area and volume measurements of various objects. For instance, an astronomer might be interested in measuring the area spanned by a spiral galaxy on a spherical patch of sky, and a medical technician might be interested in tracking the growth of a tumor by measuring its surface area or volume through X-ray imaging. The surveyor's area formula, or the shoelace formula, provides a standard combinatorial approach to determine the area or volume enclosed by a loop comprised of discrete linear piece-wise segments (i.e. tracing the outer edges of a spiral galaxy). Such formulas are easily derived from classical vector calculus techniques including Green's theorem and the Divergence theorem. A limitation of these formulas, however, is their assumption of linear or planar geometry. In applications where surface curvature matters or the surface in question is sparsely sampled, these standard techniques begin to fail and yield exceedingly inaccurate area and volume measurements. Recently, a novel approach for measuring surface area on the sphere given a collection of longitude and latitude points was introduced by utilizing the generalized Stokes theorem expressed in the language of differential forms. In the context of measuring the area enclosed by polygons on spheres, this new approach provides higher accuracy for sparsely sampled boundary data.

In this preliminary work, the generalized Stokes theorem is used to derive a new set of combinatorial formulas for area and volume measurements on various geometrical objects given data from the boundary. While the formalism developed is relatively simple, these formulas provide a novel analytical approach to accurately determine area and volume measurements on complicated surfaces.

Search for Fast Evolving Luminous Transients (FELTs)

with Masao Sako (UPenn)

FELTs are violent stellar explosions achieving peak luminosities on extremely short timescales (e.g. days to weeks). A small number of these rare transient events have been identified, with some reaching maximum luminosities comparable to Type Ia supernovae. In this analysis, we use photometric data from the Dark Energy Survey and difference imaging techniques to work towards identifying additional FELT candidates, and understanding the underlying mechanisms responsible for such ephemeral cosmic explosions.
